Net Asset Value

Net Asset Value (NAV) represents the total market worth of a portfolio. It is the sum of the market value of all held assets plus any uninvested cash on a specific date.

How is it calculated?
$$NAV_t = \text{Cash}_t + \sum_{i=1}^{n} (\text{Position}_{i,t} \times \text{Price}_{i,t})$$
Where, \(n\) is the total number of assets, \(i\) is a specific asset, and \(t\) is the specific point in time.
Why is it useful?

It provides a single, clear figure to track the performance of a portfolio over time. By calculating NAV daily, we can generate an Equity Curve, which is essential for calculating further risk metrics like Volatility, Sharpe Ratio, and Maximum Drawdown.

{{ MetricCard("Total Return", metrics.total_return, id="kpi-total-return", description="What is total return?

Total return is the full gain or loss from an investment over a period of time. It includes both the change in price and any money the investment paid out, like dividends or interest.

How is it calculated?

$$\\text{Total Return} = \\left(\\frac{\\text{Ending Value} - \\text{Starting Value} + \\text{Income}}{\\text{Starting Value}}\\right) \\times 100$$

EV = Ending Value, SV = Starting Value, I = Income. Income means things like dividends or interest. If those payouts are reinvested, they are also part of the total return.

Why is it useful?

It gives a more complete picture than price change alone. A stock may not rise much in price, but it can still give a good return if it pays dividends. That is why total return is useful for comparing investments fairly. (Banton, 2025)") }} {{ MetricCard("Total Value", metrics.total_value, id="kpi-total-value", description="What is total value?

Total value is the total worth of everything in your portfolio right now. It adds up the current value of all your investments.

How is it calculated?

$$\\text{Portfolio Value} = \\sum_{i=1}^{n} q_i \\cdot p_i$$

Why is it useful?

It gives a quick snapshot of where your portfolio stands today. It helps you track progress, compare performance over time, and decide when you may need to rebalance. (StartEngine Team, 2026)") }} {{ MetricCard("Jensen's Alpha", metrics.alpha, id="kpi-alpha", description="What is Jensen's alpha?

Jensen's alpha measures how much a portfolio beat or missed its expected return after adjusting for market risk (beta).

How is it calculated?

$$\\alpha = R_p - \\left(R_f + \\beta\\left(R_m - R_f\\right)\\right)$$

Why is it useful?

It helps judge manager skill more fairly by accounting for risk.") }} {{ MetricCard("CAGR", metrics.cagr, id="kpi-cagr", description="What is CAGR?

CAGR is the steady yearly growth rate that would take an investment from start value to end value, assuming reinvestment.

How is it calculated?

$$\\text{CAGR} = \\left(\\left(\\frac{\\text{Ending Value}}{\\text{Beginning Value}}\\right)^{\\frac{1}{\\text{Years}}} - 1\\right) \\times 100$$

Why is it useful?

It makes it easy to compare long-term growth, but it does not show volatility by itself. (CFI, 2026)") }} {{ MetricCard("Beta", metrics.beta, id="kpi-beta", description="What is beta?

Beta measures how sensitive a portfolio is to market moves.

How is it calculated?

Why is it useful?

It helps you understand market risk and match investments to your risk tolerance. (CFI, 2026)") }} {{ MetricCard("Volatility", metrics.volatility, id="kpi-volatility", description="What is volatility?

Volatility shows how much and how quickly prices move up and down over time.
